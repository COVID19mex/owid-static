Land Cover

by Max Roser and Hannah Ritchie [cite]
OWID presents work from many different people and organizations. When citing this entry, please also cite the original data source. This entry can be cited as:

Max Roser and Hannah Ritchie (2017) – ‘Land Cover’. Published online at OurWorldInData.org. Retrieved from: /land-cover/ [Online Resource]

# Empirical View

# Land Use over the Long-Run

The visualisation below shows human land use over the long-term (since 10,000 BC), and details the change in total land used for cropland, grazing land and built-up/urban area in hectares. This can also be viewed by select countries and all regions using the “change country/region” option below.

# Land Use of the World Today

The graphic below details the breakdown of global land allocation and use based on areal extent. Only 71 percent of Earth’s land surface is defined as habitable. Humans use half of global habitable area for agricultural production (of the remainder, 37 percent is forested; 11 percent as shrubbery; and only one-percent is utilised as urban infrastructure).

More than three-quarters of our agricultural land is used for the rearing of livestock through a combination of grazing land and land used for animal feed production. Despite being dominant in land allocation for agriculture, meat and dairy products supply only 17, and 33 percent of global caloric and protein supply.

Footnotes

  1. This map is published by NASA/NOAA here.

    Shown in this map is the detailed vegetation index data from the VIIRS sensor aboard the NASA/NOAA Suomi NPP satellite. Satellite data from April 2012 to April 2013 was used to generate this image. The source explains: “The darkest green areas are the lushest in vegetation, while the pale colors are sparse in vegetation cover either due to snow, drought, rock, or urban areas.”

    Two versions are available for download at the website – a 4096 x 2048 image and a super high resolution version (30,000 x 15,000) which is 176 MB in size.

  2. This is taken from the highly cited Lambin et al. (2001) – The causes of land-use and land-cover change: moving beyond the myths. Online here.

    The original source of the data is Goldewijk, K., Battjes, J.J., (1997) – A Hundred Year Database for Integrated Environmental Assessments. National Institute of Public Health and the Environment, Bilthoven.

  3. This map is taken from Wikipedia here.
    According to Wikipedia the map shows the recent ‘greening’ of the Sahel. It shows the results of trend analyses of time series over the Sahel sub-Saharan savanna region of seasonally integrated Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI).
    Areas with trends of <95% probability in white.
