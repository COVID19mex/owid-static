Population growth and famines

February 13, 2018 by Joe Hasell
Our World in Data presents the empirical evidence on global development in entries dedicated to specific topics.

This blog post draws on data and research discussed in our entry on Famines.

It’s no good blaming climate change or food shortages or political corruption. Sorry to be neo-Malthusian about it, but continuing population growth in this region makes periodic famine unavoidable – as many people have been pointing out since the last famine. Many of the children saved by the money raised over the next few weeks will inevitably be back again in similar feeding centres with their own children in a few years time.

- Blog entry by British environmentalist Sir Jonathon Porritt, www.jonathanporritt.com, 11/07/2011

According to the Famine Early Warning Systems Network, four countries – Yemen, South Sudan, Somalia, and Nigeria – face a 'credible risk' of famine in 2018. An estimated 76 million people across 45 countries will be in need of emergency food assistance, only marginally lower than the 'unprecedented' levels witnessed in 2017.

Is population growth in fact the ultimate cause of famine conditions, as the quote by Sir Jonathon Porritt asserts? Porritt is the former director of 'Friends of the Earth' and also former chairman of the UK Government’s Sustainable Development Commission and in his post he seems certain that the rapid population growth witnessed in East Africa had made famine there ‘unavoidable’.

There is something compelling about this logic: a finite planet, with a limited ‘carrying capacity’, cannot continue to feed a growing population indefinitely. As referenced in the quote above, this is the contemporary take on Malthus’ assertion that, in the absence of ‘preventative checks’ to reduce birth rates, the natural tendency for populations to increase – being ‘so superior to the power of the earth to produce subsistence for man’ – ultimately results in ‘positive checks’ that increase the death rate. If all else fails to curb population, ‘gigantic inevitable famine stalks in the rear, and with one mighty blow levels the population with the food of the world'.1

Thus, contained in this ‘Malthusian’ proposition are two hypotheses: firstly, that unchecked population growth causes famine and, secondly, that famine acts as a ‘check’ to population. Does the evidence support these assertions?

Does population growth cause famine? – Global perspective

Looking at the world as whole, it is difficult to square this hypothesis with the simple but stark fact that, despite the world population increasing from less than one billion in 1800 to more than seven billion today, the number of people dying due to famine in recent decades is only a tiny fraction of that in previous eras. For our entry on Famines we produced a dataset of the estimated number of deaths from all famines worldwide since the 1860s. The chart below compares the number of deaths per decade with the total world population over the same period.

The number of famine victims by decade, 1860s-2010s

We might naturally think that the explanation for this trend lies in increasing agricultural production. Indeed, food supply per person has consistently increased in recent decades, the large increase in global population being met with an even greater increase in food supply (largely due to increases in yields per hectare).

However, looking at the issue in this way is too simple. As we discuss in our entry on Famines, insufficient aggregate food supply per person is just one factor that can bring about famine mortality. The analysis of 'modern' famines has found that insufficient aggregate food supply is less important than one might think and instead emphasized the role of public policy and violence: In most famines of the 20th and 21st centuries, conflict, political oppression, corruption, or gross economic mismanagement on the part of dictatorships or colonial regimes played a key role. The same also applies for the most acutely food-insecure countries today2.

Thus, all in all, the recent history of famine mortality does not fit the Malthusian narrative particularly well. Firstly, food supply per person has – in all regions – increased as populations have expanded. Secondly, in the modern era the occurrence of major famine mortality, and its prevention, is something for which politics and policy seem the more salient triggers. Famine scholar Stephen Devereux of the Institute of Development Studies, University of Sussex, summarises the trajectory of famines over the 20th century as follows: "The achievement of a global capacity to guarantee food security was accompanied by a simultaneous expansion of the capacity of governments to inflict lethal policies, including genocidal policies often involving the extraction of food from the poor and denial of food to the starving."3

Does population growth increase hunger? – Country-level perspective

Famines tend to be thought of as acute periods of crisis, and are in that sense to be distinguished from more chronic manifestations of hunger that may in some places represent 'normal' circumstances.4 Given the typically political nature of outbreaks of such famine crises, it may make more sense to look for an effect of population growth on the longer-term trends of hunger and malnutrition. Do those countries with high population growth rates find it harder to adequately feed its population in general?

In order to get some idea about this, we can look at countries' Global Hunger Index score. This is a composite measure, out of 100, that combines four indicators: undernourishment, child wasting, child stunting, and child mortality.5 The first scoring was conducted in 1992, and was then repeated every eight years with the most recent being in carried out in 2017. The score is based on data collected in the years leading up to the scoring year, and as such reflect the hunger levels in this period rather than solely capturing conditions in the year itself. The graphs below show all the countries for which there was GHI data available between 1992 and 2017.6 Crucially, this excludes a number of very food-insecure countries including the Democratic Republic of Congo, South Sudan and Somalia, which have also seen high levels of population growth.7 This should be borne in mind when interpreting the following results.

Of the countries for which we do have GHI data, it is clear that those with higher levels of hunger have also tended to have had higher population growth over the last 25 years.8

It is important to see though that among the countries for which we have GHI scores in both 1992 and 2017, the level of hunger went down in all but one (Iraq). Over the same period population went up in almost every case. Moreover, those countries that experienced higher levels of population growth in fact saw a bigger drop in their GHI score over this period.9

But what could explain this relationship between high population growth rates and bigger decreases in hunger? What we find is that those countries that had high population growth over this period are the countries with high levels of hunger in 1992. So what we are seeing here is that countries are converging towards lower levels of hunger: Hunger fell quickest in countries with the highest levels of hunger, as we can see in the chart below.

Thus the idea that rapid population growth inevitably brings about increased hunger clearly does not easily fit the picture painted by this set of data. Amongst the countries for which data was available, levels of hunger have fallen almost universally and they have fallen quickest in those countries with the highest levels of hunger – despite their having higher population growth. It may be that rapid population growth can make countries more likely to experience hunger relative to other countries, or even relative to what might have been achievable itself with a lower population growth rate. But these data do cast doubt on the idea that population growth inevitably leads to increased hunger in absolute terms.

Do famines 'check' population?

Over the course of decades, long-term changes in birth, death and emigration rates far outweigh the demographic impacts of even the most severe famines.

A striking example of this is the 'Great Leap Forward' famine in China in 1959-61, the single biggest famine in terms of absolute numbers of deaths. The chart below shows the demographic changes over the course of the famine: As is common, the famine itself produced a fall in the number of births. However, this was immediately followed by a spike in the birth rate in the years immediately following the famine, offsetting to a large degree its demographic effect. Despite causing an excess mortality of 2-5% of the total population, and a similar number of 'lost births', when we zoom out to look at the long run population trend, we see the famine had next to no discernible impact. Over the long run, the secular decline in fertility rates witnessed in China – along with the rest of of the world – played a much larger role in limiting population growth.

Long-term demographic impact of China's Great Leap Forward Famine10

This picture contrasts somewhat with the developments following the Great Irish Famine of the late 1840s, shown in the chart below. After a period of rapid growth that brought the population to over 8 million, a famine struck that was, in relation to the country's population, far more severe than the Great Leap Forward famine. It is thought to have reduced the population by about one quarter in its immediate effects: one million perishing and a further million emigrating.

But one striking feature of Irish demographics is how the population then continued to shrink. By 1911 there were in Ireland about half as many people as in 1841. The population only began to grow again in the late 20th century. This continued depopulation was partly due to low birth rates (which were considerably lower than in England and Wales in the second half of the 19th century) and partly due to very high levels of outward migration. Between 1851 and 1900, there were almost as many outward migrants as there were deaths in Ireland (4.18 million and 4.56 million, respectively)11.

The only way a famine can have any real lasting demographic impact is if it "teaches" the population to alter marriage and family planning practices to reduce fertility rates. Whilst there is some evidence of behaviour change in Ireland following the famine, particularly in terms of the age and frequency with which people married, it is difficult to disentangle any “teaching” effect in this from other factors such as the increasing life expectancy and incomes enjoyed in the decades following. Furthermore, whilst total birth rates were low, the number of children being born to married couples remained high in the post-famine period, which complicates any simple explanation along these lines. Similarly, whilst the famine itself clearly provided the impetus for mass emigration, high levels of outward migration began some decades before the famine and continued long afterwards in the context of a much-ameliorated standard of living. Thus, it seems likely that it was the promise of improved economic opportunities, rather than fear of famine which drove emigration between 1851 and 190012.

Overall then, even in this seemingly paradigmatic ‘Malthusian’ example, whilst Ireland undoubtedly did suffer some lasting demographic impacts from the famine, subsequent economic and social developments unrelated to the famine explain the majority of the depopulation the country experienced in the decades following it.

Conclusions

Whilst growing populations clearly can increase pressures on the environment in many ways, it is important to realise that there is nothing inevitable about famine in the 21st century – regardless of level of population growth. Moreover, famines tend to have a fairly negligible demographic effect in the long run, being far outweighed by the secular shifts seen in the demographic transition towards low mortality rates followed by low fertility rates. In terms of more chronic manifestations of hunger, those countries with high population growth do tend to have higher levels of hunger but have in recent years seen larger falls in that level, showing that improvements in people's diets are not at all precluded by population growth.

All in all, anyone tempted to view famine today as being either an inevitable consequence of, or a ‘cure’ for population growth, and therefore in some sense morally acceptable, needs to be aware that both hypotheses do not stand up to much scrutiny.
