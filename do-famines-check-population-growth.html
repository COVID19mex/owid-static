Do famines 'check' population growth?

February 23, 2018 by Joe Hasell
Our World in Data presents the empirical evidence on global development in entries dedicated to specific topics.

This blog post draws on data and research discussed in our entry on Famines.

This entry is the second of a pair of entries considering the relationship between famines and population growth. In the first we looked at whether population growth causes famine and hunger. Here we consider the demographic impacts of famines, and in particular the extent to which famines may 'check' population growth.

Do famines 'check' population growth?

Nineteenth-century English political economist, Thomas Robert Malthus, is famous for framing famine as "the last, the most dreadful resource of nature" in the face of sustained growth of the human population population which, left to its own devices, naturally tends to outstrip agricultural production.1

Contained in this ‘Malthusian’ proposition are two hypotheses: firstly, that rapid population growth, if 'unchecked', leads to famine; secondly, that famine acts as a ‘check’ to population in this eventuality. We saw in the previous entry that the first is highly problematic.....

Over the course of decades, long-term changes in birth, death and emigration rates far outweigh the demographic impacts of even the most severe famines.

A striking example of this is the 'Great Leap Forward' famine in China in 1959-61, the single biggest famine in terms of absolute numbers of deaths. The chart below shows the demographic changes over the course of the famine: As is common, the famine itself produced a fall in the number of births. However, this was immediately followed by a spike in the birth rate in the years immediately following the famine, offsetting to a large degree its demographic effect. Despite causing an excess mortality of 2-5% of the total population, and a similar number of 'lost births', when we zoom out to look at the long run population trend, we see the famine had next to no discernible impact. Over the long run, the secular decline in fertility rates witnessed in China – along with the rest of of the world – played a much larger role in limiting population growth.

Long-term demographic impact of China's Great Leap Forward Famine2

This picture contrasts somewhat with the developments following the Great Irish Famine of the late 1840s, shown in the chart below. After a period of rapid growth that brought the population to over 8 million, a famine struck that was, in relation to the country's population, far more severe than the Great Leap Forward famine. It is thought to have reduced the population by about one quarter in its immediate effects: one million perishing and a further million emigrating.

But one striking feature of Irish demographics is how the population then continued to shrink. By 1911 there were in Ireland about half as many people as in 1841. The population only began to grow again in the late 20th century. This continued depopulation was partly due to low birth rates (which were considerably lower than in England and Wales in the second half of the 19th century) and partly due to very high levels of outward migration. Between 1851 and 1900, there were almost as many outward migrants as there were deaths in Ireland (4.18 million and 4.56 million, respectively)3.

The only way a famine can have any real lasting demographic impact is if it "teaches" the population to alter marriage and family planning practices to reduce fertility rates. Whilst there is some evidence of behaviour change in Ireland following the famine, particularly in terms of the age and frequency with which people married, it is difficult to disentangle any “teaching” effect in this from other factors such as the increasing life expectancy and incomes enjoyed in the decades following. Furthermore, whilst total birth rates were low, the number of children being born to married couples remained high in the post-famine period, which complicates any simple explanation along these lines. Similarly, whilst the famine itself clearly provided the impetus for mass emigration, high levels of outward migration began some decades before the famine and continued long afterwards in the context of a much-ameliorated standard of living. Thus, it seems likely that it was the promise of improved economic opportunities, rather than fear of famine which drove emigration between 1851 and 19004.

Overall then, even in this seemingly paradigmatic ‘Malthusian’ example, whilst Ireland undoubtedly did suffer some lasting demographic impacts from the famine, subsequent economic and social developments unrelated to the famine explain the majority of the depopulation the country experienced in the decades following it.

famines tend to have a fairly negligible demographic effect in the long run, being far outweighed by the secular shifts seen in the demographic transition towards low mortality rates followed by low fertility rates. In terms of more chronic manifestations of hunger, those countries with high population growth do tend to have higher levels of hunger but have in recent years seen larger falls in that level, showing that improvements in people's diets are not at all precluded by population growth.

O Grada mentions a handful of studies that see some short-term behaviour change in terms of age of marriage/family planning.
