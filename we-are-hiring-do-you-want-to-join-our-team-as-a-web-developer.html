We are hiring! Do you want to join our team as a web developer?

March 05, 2019 by Max Roser

We from Our World in Data are right now at Y Combinator in Silicon Valley as one of the few academic non-profits that were ever selected. The experience here helped us to develop a very clear vision for our work in the coming year and it made clear that we can do better if we find one more colleague to work with us on.

Thanks to the direct support from readers – via donations – and via academic grants we now also have stable funding for the next years, so we're looking for one more colleague:

  • A frontend developer with strong full-stack development skills who will focus on making Our World in Data better for the reader and who will help us build a new visual exploration tool to allow the readers access to our database to explore the tens of thousands of datasets the research team brought together. You can apply here at the site of the University of Oxford where we are based.

You can find all the details about the positions in the links above. But I wanted to take the chance to tell you a bit more about my own experience with the project. I am Max Roser who started working on this publication in 2012.

In the development team you will join two excellent developers: Jaiden Mispy, who has been working on OWID since March 2016, and Daniel Gavrilov, who joined us last year.

About Our World in Data

Our World in Data is a non-profit, creative commons web project based at the University of Oxford. We write articles about the world's biggest problems and how we can make a difference. We are interested in how living conditions and the earth's environment are changing and why. We cover global poverty, CO2 emissions, fertility rates, mental health, and many more topics. Our role is basically to act as a bridge between the scientific literature and the rest of the world, presenting information in a way that is understandable by non-specialist audiences while still being rigorous and in depth.

I started back in 2012 by myself, but since then we've grown to become a proper team of researchers, writers, and programmers. We've been covered widely by the media, and often work on collaborations with a wide reach and great partners, for example videos (1, 2, 3) with Kurzgesagt or books or big policy institutions.

While much of academic research is pretty published in a format that was available to Gutenberg in the 15th century (pages with text and some visuals), we try to go beyond that and make use of the opportunities of the web to make data and research available. This is where programming comes in. We have a big database with some 70,000+ variables imported from many different sources. Our authors then use our chart authoring tool to take that data and create an interactive visualization to embed, like so:

By reading this chart and using the interactive mouseover, you can quickly come to know some important facts about the world:

  • As of 2015, about 1 in 20 of all children die before the age of five: a tragic loss of life on an immense scale.
  • There is a lot of regional inequality involved. We see that the child mortality rate as of 2015 is highest in Africa and lowest in Europe.
  • Finally, we see that this problem was much worse in the past. In Asia, the child mortality rate has dropped from a quarter in 1950 to 3.52% in 2015.

Take a moment to think about what those numbers mean in human reality: the millions upon millions of lives involved, both lost and saved. It is sobering. The hope is that we can help in some small way to prevent such tragedies by collating research into their nature and causation, and this motivates much of what we do.

So, how does this visualization look under the hood? Essentially, it is a "window" into our database defined by a JSON configuration. This configuration is created by our chart editor and specifies what kind of visualization, which variables from the database to request, and which countries ("entities") should be shown, along with any text annotation. The config and data are then given to my little visualization engine (written in TypeScript, based around React and Mobx) to create the final SVG result.

The "Add country"-button works by locally altering the JSON config to change the specified entities, kind of like a mini version of our chart editor. Combinatorially speaking, the number of potential states that embed can be in is vast! I think it's something like 2×1029. If a picture is worth a thousand words, an interactive visualization can potentially be worth a thousand pictures.

This big database and visualization system grew organically as we needed to produce lots of charts very quickly. Now that we have both long-term funding and all this data available on our platform, there's a lot more we can do with it-- which is why we are looking for you. For example, some projects we have planned:

  • Build a public visualization interface for freely exploring the full database (not just individual embeds)
  • Create a searchable list of all of our content by topic: entries, blog posts, interactive and static visualizations
  • Make nicely-designed pages for individual countries going over their progress towards the Sustainable Development Goals
  • Improve the discoverability of our long entries – for example this one on population growth
  • Implement new types of interactive visualizations: stacked bar charts, cumulative distribution plots, Sankey diagrams
  • Release our visualization frontend code as a standalone JS library on NPM for others to use (it's already open source, but it needs cleaning up)

We are all pretty excited to work on those, and hopefully you are too!

Working at Oxford

Our team is distributed around the world and we communicate via Slack and Basecamp, but our home is the University of Oxford. If you're physically nearby it is a lovely city and university to work at (it's hard to tell where the university ends and the city begins), but remote work in some cases might also be possible, get in touch.

OWID being based out of the university means we aren't primarily a tech organization. We're not a startup and there's no venture capital involved, our team is one half scientist and one half developers. We take both sides very seriously: Here at Y Combinator we are at a great place for the development team and we are building many contacts from here; back at the University of Oxford we are at home in one of the best places in the world to do research.

Our main users – the readers of our work – are great. It is very motivating to work for an enthusiastic and growing audience. Though there's the occasional inevitable internet negativity, the feedback we get is often a stream of positive feedback. Here are three from the last 4 days:

From feedback, we know that our work is used in lots of different ways: by journalists, professors, school teachers, charities in low-income countries, and even one or two psychologists who have included global development data in therapy sessions (we were very surprised about that one!).

For all of us it is  incredibly motivating to know that our work is useful to other people, and that seems to be the case for most people. So I will leave that as my main argument for why you should come work with us.

If you don't want to take my word for it, maybe you trust Patrick Collison's judgement? Patrick permanently recommends working with us on his job recommendation page.

If you want join us as our new design-focused frontend developer, be sure to get in touch and apply!
